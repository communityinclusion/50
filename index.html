50 Years of Inclusion

Some 50 years ago, the first elements of today's Institute for Community Inclusion, were formed as the Developmental Evaluation Clinic (At Boston Children's Hospital). In the years following, the modern ICI blossomed into leading disability focused training and research organization that you see today. Join us for a year of special events, celebrating 50 years of inclusion. To keep updated on our developing schedule, feel free join our mailing list, follow us on social media, and check in on our events calendar.

  • 1961–1963 President John F. Kennedy appoints the "President's Panel on Mental Retardation" in 1961. The Panel’s 1962 report contains many recommendations, including research, preventive health measures, strengthened educational programs, more comprehensive and improved clinical and social services, improved methods and facilities for care, a new legal and social concept of mental retardation, increased educational opportunities to learn about mental retardation, and public education and information programs. The Panel’s report prompts the development of new legislation that authorizes funding for developmental research centers in university-affiliated facilities and community facilities for people with mental retardation. A national network is launched of University Centers for Excellence in Developmental Disabilities, or UCEDDs. Primarily based at public, land-grant universities, the UCEDDs provide a new national base for academic research into developmental disabilities. Amendments to the Social Security Act increase services for maternal and child health, and fund studies in each state on the status of services for people with mental retardation.
  • 1964 Children's Hospital (now Boston Children's Hospital) obtains funding for constructing 2 floors in the new Fegan Building to be devoted to clinical services for persons with developmental disabilities.
  • 1967 The Developmental Evaluation Clinic (DEC) at Children's Hospital opens its doors on the 10th and 11th floors of the Fegan Building. Dr. Allen C. Crocker is the founder and first director of the DEC, which later grows into the Institute for Community Inclusion (ICI). The ICI is part of the national network of University Centers for Excellence in Developmental Disabilities (UCEDDs) that was begun by President Kennedy (see above).
  • 1969Grants that provide operational funding for the DEC are received from the federal departments of Maternal and Child Health and Social Rehabilitation Services. Collaboration is started with 2 Commonwealth of Massachusetts agencies: the Bureau of Education for the Handicapped, and the Department of Mental Health.
  • 1969Staffing grows to 17 professionals representing 10 disciplines. Collaboration with area universities is started, and the DEC provides training for their graduate–postgraduate students in variety of disciplines.
  • 1969-72 Clinical and training programs grow. “Core” training curriculum is developed. Clinical services include comprehensive “primary evaluations” delivered by interdisciplinary teams to 4–8 children a week.
  • 1969-72 “Down I” program combines research and clinical services for young children (starting with newborns) who have Down syndrome. A demonstration preschool classroom is opened on Fegan 10 for children with disabilities in collaboration with the Massachusetts Department of Education and Mental Health.
  • 1972 William (Bill) Kiernan, current dean of the School for Global Inclusion and Social Development at UMass Boston, and also director of ICI from 198? to 2016, is hired as a research associate at Children's Hospital.
  • 1972 Massachusetts Chapter 766 is passed, guaranteeing all children the right to a free, appropriate public education. It is the first USA special education law, and becomes the model for later federal legislation. The ICI, in partnership with the state, provides training for special education teachers, and “primary evaluation” meetings serve as model for school individualized education program (IEP) meetings.
  • 1976 The Children's Hospital Work Experience Program (WEP) begins. The original idea for the WEP came from Sally Yateman, a social worker at the DEC, along with Bill Kiernan, as an alternative to summer camp for young adults in the Boston Public Schools. The WEP provides intensive training and supervision for young adults with disabilities who are placed in various departments at Children’s Hospital. Regular supportive groups are conducted for them by ICI vocational rehabilitation students and staff, led jointly with a DEC psychiatrist. This program continues today, providing people with disabilities opportunities for career exploration and the development of new skills to support them in achieving their employment goals.
  • 1976 ICI receives state contract for developing modern medical services at the Wrentham State School (WSS) (now called Wrentham Developmental Center), following a court ruling in a class action suit on behalf on the residents. Virtually all disciplines participate, especially medicine, nursing, psychiatry, and physical therapy. Additional staff is hired to provide on-site services and staff training. The conditions at WSS are markedly improved, and eventually court supervision is terminated. However, Dr. Norberto Alvarez, originally an early ICI trainee in neuropsychiatry, still works at Wrentham Developmental Center as a medical director. We need to check on this.
  • 1981-82 DEC receives supported work contract from Bay State Skills Corporation, a quasi-state agency charged with implementing supported work programs across the state. The programs provide job training, work experience, and job placement for people with disabilities.
  • 1987 The DEC’s growing vocational rehabilitation team and employment researchers move to the Gardner House on the BCH campus. This unit becomes known as the Training and Research Institute for People with Disabilities (TRIPD).
  • 1987 TRIPD staff works with the Boston College (BC) Campus School to start the BC Supported Employment Program.
  • 1988 The Access to Integrated Employment project begins, funded by the Administration on Developmental Disabilities (now the Administration on Intellectual and Developmental Disabilities). The project innovates by conducting large nationwide surveys about the employment outcomes of people with intellectual and developmental disabilities. The first survey started earlier than this I think - Jean Cibirowski worked with BIll on it and we were still on Fegan 10.
  • 1990 IThe TRIPD establishes its first office at UMass Boston.
  • 1990 The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) is passed.
  • 1990 The Center for Autism and Related Disabilities opens at Children’s Hospital, directed by Dr. Szymanski.
  • 1992The Training and Research Institute for People with Disabilities (TRIPD) is officially renamed the Institute for Community Inclusion (ICI).
  • 1993 Bill Kiernan is appointed the director of ICI.
  • 1993 ICI receives a federal grant to start its first Rehabilitation Research and Training Center, a 5-year project called the Center on Promoting Employment.
  • 1996Community capacity-building projects (13 total) are created to develop long-term partnerships with at least 29 diverse community-based organizations in Massachusetts, signaling ICI’s active commitment to cultural competence and supporting underrepresented populations.
  • 1998 The Vision Rehabilitation Therapy program is funded. This program provides training and a graduate degree for professionals entering the field of Vision Studies, and will grow into the Vision Studies MEd at UMass Boston.
  • 2000 IThe National Service Inclusion Project is created, providing training and technical assistance to support individuals with disabilities participating in AmeriCorps, Senior Corps, and service learning opportunities in all states and territories. For over 15 years, ICI received 4 competitive grant awards to fund this project.
  • 2001 The majority of ICI staff become UMass Boston employees.
  • 2002ICI receives the National Center on Workforce Development grant from the federal Office of Disability Employment Policy, or ODEP.
  • 2002 ICI moves to the Park Plaza Building in Boston’s Back Bay neighborhood.
  • 2003 IThe Think College is formally named, having grown from a 3-year grant from the federal Office of Special Education in 1997 in partnership with 5 Massachusetts community colleges. The project focuses on improving access to higher education for young people with intellectual disabilities.
  • 2005The State Employment Leadership Network (SELN) is established. This membership organization provides a forum for state developmental disabilities agencies to collaborate and receive technical assistance from ICI as they work to improve employment outcomes for people with intellectual and developmental disabilities.
  • 2006 Early Childhood is added to ICI's network. (more info?)
  • 2010 ICI’s National Service Inclusion Project is awarded Engaging Veterans with Disabilities Initiative to enhance the capacity of national service and volunteer programs to engage and support veterans with disabilities.
  • 2011 The founding director of the DEC, Dr. Allen Crocker, passes away at 85 years old.
  • 2012 The School for Global Inclusion and Social Development (SGISD) at UMass Boston is established, with Bill Kiernan as founding dean.
  • 2012ICI expands its international portfolio. In 2012, ICI staff member Dr. Heike Boeltzig-Brown relocates to Tokyo, Japan. Together with U.S.-based staffers Miwa Tanabe and Cate Weir, Dr. Boeltzig-Brown creates several disability leadership programs targeting Japanese youth with disabilities, as well as disability and career services professionals from Japanese universities. She also develops partnerships with Japanese academics and researchers to explore disability inclusion in higher education and the labor market cross-nationally.
  • 2014 SGISD begins accepting students.
  • 2015 ICI moves to its current location at the Bayside Office Building, 150 Mt. Vernon Street, at Columbia Point, Dorchester.
  • 2016 ICI expands its international portfolio. ICI and the Association of University Centers on Disabilities win a grant from the U.S. Department of State to establish the ADA International Fellowship Program. The program supports 20 mid-career professionals from Brazil, Kenya, Peru, and Tanzania to participate in a 5-week intensive fellowship program at a university-based center for disabilities in the U.S.
  • 2017 Tom Sannicandro becomes the new ICI director.
  • 2018 ICI expands its international portfolio:ICI, the Association of University Centers on Disabilities, and Humanity & Inclusion win a grant from the U.S. Department of State to establish the Professional Fellows Program for Inclusive Disability Employment. The program supports 20 mid-career professionals from Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda who are committed to advancing inclusive employment for individuals with disabilities. Fellows participate in an intensive 5-week program at a university-based center for disabilities in the U.S.
